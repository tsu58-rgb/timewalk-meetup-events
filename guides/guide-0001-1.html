Most visitors know Koenji and Asagaya for music, shopping streets, and residential Tokyo. Few realize that the area also contains a compact temple district created partly by the rebuilding of the modern capital. During the early twentieth century, railways, road expansion, land readjustment, and changing urban conditions pushed religious institutions out of central neighborhoods such as Asakusa, Ueno, Azabu, Ushigome, and Shinjuku. Several of those temples reassembled here. This self-guided walk follows ten sites over about three kilometers, using relocated temples, old village institutions, graves, and local traditions to explain how central Tokyo’s transformation reshaped suburban Suginami.

Why Umezato Has a Temple Town

The cluster was not created by a single disaster. Some temples arrived before the 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake because railway construction, road widening, land readjustment, damp ground, or other pressures made their former locations difficult to maintain. Several moved between 1909 and 1920. The earthquake and the 1945 air raids then damaged or destroyed buildings and religious objects, producing further cycles of rebuilding. Two locally founded temples near the end of the walk show that the district is also rooted in older village history. The result is not a preserved fragment of Edo, but a landscape assembled through movement, loss, adaptation, and continuity.

Before You Go

These are active places of worship, not open-air museum exhibits. Stay out of private or restricted areas, keep voices low, and do not photograph graves, ceremonies, interiors, or worshippers without permission. Individual graves and cultural objects may be difficult to locate or unavailable to view. Opening conditions can change for funerals, memorial services, construction, or temple events. Use the historical explanations even when a specific object cannot be seen, and treat every cemetery visit with particular care.

Ten Stops on the Umezato Temple Town Walk

1. Saiho-ji: A Temple Displaced by Rail and Road Expansion

Begin with the physical force that helped create this temple district. Saiho-ji originally stood at Yotsuya Oiwake, near the north side of present-day Shinjuku Station. It moved here in 1920 because the Chuo Line and nearby roads were being widened—not because of the 1923 earthquake. In the grounds, look for a six-sided stone Kannon pillar dated 1653. Suginami Ward designates it a tangible cultural property and notes its rarity and value as evidence of early Edo-period Koshin belief. The temple buildings were destroyed in May 1945 and rebuilt in 1981.

2. Seitoku-ji: The Red Gate Temple from Azabu

Seitoku-ji was founded in Azabu in 1629 and relocated to Umezato in 1913, when the temple sought a new site for future development. This move predated the 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake, so it belongs to an earlier phase of temple relocation than many sites on the route. The name Akamon-dera—“Red Gate Temple”—comes from a vermilion gate the temple originally had; the official account does not state that this gate survives. After repeated fires in Azabu, the temple was damaged again by the 1923 earthquake and the Pacific War, followed by restoration and improvement work from 1965 onward.

3. Chiko-in: From Ueno and Asakusa to Suburban Suginami

Chiko-in is said to have been founded in 1630 beside Kanei-ji in Ueno. It moved to Asakusa in 1698, and its main hall later collapsed in the 1855 Edo earthquake. The temple relocated to its present Suginami site in 1912 because of land readjustment. Its cemetery contains the grave of Nagata Masamichi, who served as an Edo north magistrate during the Bunka and Bunsei eras (1804–1830). Chiko-in also preserves a stone memorial tablet dated to 1460–1466 and a wooden seated Jizo made in 1655; its present main hall was moved here from Fukuzoin in Nakano Ward.
