4. Keian-ji: Maeno Ryotaku and the World of Dutch Learning

Keian-ji was founded at Ikenohata beside Shinobazu Pond in 1627. The view of Ueno’s autumn maples reflected in the pond gave the temple its nickname Momiji-dera, or “Maple Temple.” Damp conditions caused severe decay and made the cemetery unsuitable, leading to its relocation to Umezato in 1913. The complex, records, and treasures were destroyed in the air raid of May 25, 1945, and the temple was rebuilt in 1966. Its cemetery contains the locally registered grave of Maeno Ryotaku, who worked with Sugita Genpaku and three others on the translation published as Kaitai Shinsho.

5. Shingetsu-in: The Kuki Family Connection—and What Moved Away

Shingetsu-in was founded in Hachobori in 1611 and moved to Asakusa in 1635. It became the family temple of the Kuki lords of the Ayabe domain after a Kuki family burial there in 1691. When district readjustment forced the temple to relocate to Umezato in 1913, the Kuki family moved to Wakayama and transferred its graves there. The connection is historically important, but the former Kuki graveyard is not a present-day attraction at this stop. Fires in 1851 and the May 1945 air raid also destroyed old records, limiting what can now be reconstructed.

6. Daiho-ji: A Grave from Edo’s Popular Publishing World

Daiho-ji is a Nichiren Buddhist temple founded in Ushigome in 1631. After fires in 1663 and again late in the Edo period, it was rebuilt in 1692 with support from members of the Asaka family. The temple moved to Suginami in 1909, and its present main hall dates from 1978. Its cemetery contains the grave of Shotei Kinsui (1795–1862), a writer of ninjobon and other popular fiction. This stop connects the temple town with the commercial publishing culture read by Edo townspeople.

7. Ketoku-in: The Legacy of Edo’s Three Great Enma

Ketoku-in is a Tendai Buddhist temple whose origin is traditionally attributed to the monk Ennin in present-day Sano. After moving through Kasumigaseki and Kuramae, it became known as the Kuramae Enma Hall and was counted among Edo’s Three Great Enma images. Its cemetery moved to Suginami in 1913, and a main hall was built here in 1929. The earlier Enma and related statues were destroyed in the 1923 earthquake. A replacement Enma image from Rinno-ji survived the 1945 destruction of the rebuilt Kuramae hall and is now enshrined at Ketoku-in.

8. Seiken-ji: Where Village History Became Modern Education

Seiken-ji changes the story. It was founded in this area in the early seventeenth century rather than moved here from central Tokyo. Its position beside the Ome Kaido reflects the older village landscape beneath modern Suginami. From 1875 to 1884, the site housed Momozono School’s First Branch, which became Toya School in 1876 and is a predecessor of today’s Suginami Daiichi Elementary School. The stop shows how temples could function not only as religious sites but also as early spaces for modern local education.

9. Kaiun-ji: A Gate Said to Come from Edo Castle

Kaiun-ji was founded in Hatchobori in 1611, moved to Asakusa in 1635, and relocated to its present Suginami site in 1910 during land readjustment. Suginami Ward describes its gate as a historic structure said to have been transferred from Momijiyama within Edo Castle. Because the official account uses “said to have been,” this guide treats the connection as a tradition rather than a proven architectural provenance. The gate nevertheless captures the walk’s central theme: Tokyo’s history survives through relocated buildings, institutions, and memories.

10. Tenkei-ji: The Okabe Family and the Name Suginami

Finish at Tenkei-ji, founded around 1633 by Okabe Yoshimasa after he became lord of Tabata village. The cemetery contains graves of successive generations of the Okabe family, designated tangible cultural properties of Suginami Ward. The ward’s official account says that the name Suginami is traditionally linked to a cedar avenue the family planted along the Ome Kaido; this guide presents that connection as a local tradition rather than a proven etymology. Ending here places the relocated temple town beside an older village institution, showing how modern Suginami grew from both local history and institutions displaced from central Tokyo.

What This Walk Reveals About Tokyo

Umezato’s temple town is valuable because it does not tell a simple story of survival in place. Temples moved from the old city to its expanding edge. Family graves sometimes moved again. Buildings and images were destroyed, rebuilt, replaced, or surrounded by new streets. At the same time, older village institutions continued beside the newcomers. Walking from Shin-koenji to Minami-asagaya therefore reveals modern Tokyo as a city assembled from relocation as much as construction. The temples are not isolated monuments; together they form a map of how the capital redistributed its memory.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this an official walking route?

No. It is an independent self-guided walk reconstructed from official Suginami City historical materials. The numbered order is a suggested sequence and may be changed.

How long does the walk take?

Allow about 90–120 minutes for roughly three kilometers, not including long stops, ceremonies, or time spent locating graves and cultural objects.

Do I need a paid map service?

No. The overview uses Leaflet with Geospatial Information Authority of Japan tiles. Each stop also has a standard Google Maps coordinate link for orientation.

Can every grave or cultural object be seen?

No. Some objects are inside buildings, difficult to identify, restricted, or unavailable during funerals, memorial services, construction, or temple events.

Is the line on the map a walking route?

No. It only connects the stops in the article’s suggested order. Choose actual streets and crossings according to current local conditions.

Is this mainly a Great Kanto Earthquake walk?

No. Several temples moved before 1923. The larger theme is how railways, road expansion, land readjustment, disaster, war, and reconstruction redistributed institutions across Tokyo.
