Koenji is usually introduced in English through vintage clothing, live music, bars, and Awa Odori. This walk looks underneath that image. It follows a river that disappeared below the streets, village shrines and stone monuments that remember an agricultural landscape, and a temple district assembled when modern Tokyo displaced religious institutions from the center. The result is a self-guided history walk that explains how Koenji was made—not a list of famous attractions.

Walk at a Glance

  • Start and finish: Koenji Station
  • Approximate distance: 5 km
  • Suggested time: 2.5–3 hours
  • Number of stops: 10
  • Best for: repeat visitors, urban-history readers, and travelers interested in lost rivers
  • Cost: free, except personal purchases
  • Difficulty: mostly level urban streets; crossings and access conditions vary

How to Use This Walk

The numbered order is a suggested narrative sequence, not a required route. You may skip or reorder stops. The map line only connects article points and is not navigation. Use current streets, crossings, signs, and access information. Religious sites are active places of worship, and some objects may be difficult to identify or unavailable.

Why a Lost River and a Temple Town Belong in the Same Walk

The Momozono River supported rice cultivation but became flood-prone and polluted as the district urbanized. Its burial created a linear public space that still records the old valley. Nearby, road widening, military expansion, fire, and land pressure moved temples out of central Tokyo and concentrated several in Koenji. The river line and temple cluster are two sides of the same process: infrastructure did not merely build modern Tokyo; it redistributed water, memory, and institutions.

Before You Go

Do not enter graves, buildings, or private areas without permission. Keep voices low and avoid blocking worshippers or residents. The greenway and roads remain everyday neighborhood spaces. Carry water, use public toilets where available, and treat coordinates as orientation points rather than guaranteed entrances.

Ten Stops on the Lost Momozono River and Koenji History Walk

The sequence below is the single source of truth for headings, map pins, anchors, and the map list.

1. Koenji Koshinzuka: Folk Belief at the Edge of the Shopping Streets

Begin north of the station at a small Koshin monument that preserves the religious landscape of former Koenji village. Koshin belief blended ideas from Daoism, Buddhism, and local communal practice; stone towers were often raised by neighborhood groups that gathered through the night on Koshin days. The monument also explains the name of nearby Koshin-dori. It is easy to pass without noticing, which makes it a useful first lesson for the walk: Koenji’s older history often survives at the scale of a street corner.

2. Koenji Hikawa Shrine: A Village Guardian and Japan’s Weather Shrine

Koenji Hikawa Shrine was a guardian shrine of the old village. Its most unusual feature is the Weather Shrine, dedicated to Yagokoro Omoikane. The shrine originated inside the Imperial Japanese Army’s meteorological unit during the war and was transferred here after 1945 at the request of people connected with the unit. This layered site links village worship, wartime science, and postwar memory before the route turns toward the vanished river.

3. Momozono River Greenway: Walking on a Buried River

The greenway follows part of the former Momozono River. The stream rose around Benten Pond in Amanuma, crossed Asagaya, Mabashi, and Koenji, and eventually joined the Kanda River. It supplied irrigation water, but its shallow channel repeatedly flooded as the district urbanized and wastewater increased. Suginami placed the river in a sewer and opened a park above it in 1969; the present greenway was completed after later improvements. Look at the long, narrow public space as the surviving line of the river rather than as an ordinary park.

4. Tanaka Inari: A Shrine that Remembers the Rice Fields

Tanaka Inari stood among rice paddies beside the Momozono River, which is why it came to be called the “Inari in the fields.” Its origin is uncertain, but it served as a guardian of farming households in old Koenji village and is an auxiliary shrine of Koenji Tenso Shrine. The approach also preserves the memory of Horinouchi Shindo, a local road used by worshippers heading toward Myoho-ji. Here the buried river becomes a map of former agriculture and pilgrimage.

5. Koenji Tenso Shrine: The Southern Guardian of the Old Village

Koenji Tenso Shrine is traditionally said to have begun when a villager brought back a divided spirit from Ise. It guarded the southern part of the old settlement, while Hikawa Shrine served another part of Koenji. The shrine offers a counterpoint to the relocated temples ahead: it represents a local institution rooted in the village landscape rather than one transplanted from central Tokyo. Treat the early foundation date as shrine tradition, not independently proven chronology.

6. Five Stone Towers: What Ordinary Villagers Prayed For

Five stone monuments survive here: Koshin towers dated 1694 and 1713, Amida towers dated 1670 and 1721, and a memorial tower dated 1728. They were raised when this area was still Koenji village, probably with prayers against disease and for community safety. The group is more revealing than a single famous monument because it shows how repeated acts of local devotion accumulated along everyday roads.

7. Choryu-ji: A Temple Moved by Military Expansion

Choryu-ji is a Soto Zen temple traditionally founded in Kojimachi in 1593. It later moved to Ichigaya and then came to Koenji in 1909 when the Imperial Japanese Army expanded its academy. A main hall built in 1756 and a gate built in 1737 were moved with the temple. The stop makes the formation of Koenji’s temple district concrete: old buildings and institutions were physically displaced by the needs of the modern capital.

8. Saisho-ji: Four Sites Across a Changing Tokyo

Saisho-ji’s history is a sequence of urban relocations. Tradition places its founding in Hibiya in 1574. Edo Castle construction pushed it to Shiba in 1612; fire and government land use led to another move; and the temple later established itself in Shirokane. In the early twentieth century it relocated to Koenji. Its repeated moves show that the temple town was not created by one disaster alone but by centuries of fire, state building, land pressure, and modernization.

9. Horin-ji: From Ushigome to Koenji’s Temple Cluster

Horin-ji was founded in 1558 near the Ushigome gate and later moved to the Ushigome temple district. Road widening reduced that site, so the temple moved to Koenji in 1914. The present neighborhood contains several temples within a short distance; Horin-ji helps explain why. Modern road construction did not simply erase old religious sites—it concentrated some of them in new suburban clusters.

10. Koen-ji Temple: The Place that Gave the District Its Name

End at Koen-ji, a Soto Zen temple founded in 1555. Suginami’s official account says the district took its name from this temple and preserves a tradition that the third Tokugawa shogun, Iemitsu, stopped here while hawking. The earlier principal image and hall were lost in an 1847 fire. Ending here brings the walk back from the twentieth-century temple town to the older institution whose name now identifies the station, the neighborhood, and the walk itself.

What This Walk Reveals About Koenji

The walk begins with small traces of village belief, crosses the buried line of a working river, and enters a district shaped by institutions moved out of central Tokyo. Koenji’s modern identity did not replace its older landscapes completely. The river survives as a green corridor, farming as a shrine name, village religion as stone monuments, and urban displacement as a temple cluster. The familiar neighborhood of shops and music sits on top of these earlier systems.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this an official route?

No. It is an independent English self-guided walk reconstructed from official Suginami materials.

Can I see the former river?

The water is underground. The greenway, narrow linear space, bridges remembered in place names, and surrounding valley help reveal its line.

How long should I allow?

About 2.5–3 hours for roughly 5 km, longer if you pause at temples or shops.

Is the dashed map line a walking route?

No. It shows article order only.

Are all temples open to visitors?

No. Access can change during ceremonies, funerals, construction, and private events.

Can I shorten the walk?

Yes. Skip the eastern shrine-and-stone section or choose only two of the three temple-town stops.
